Methodology & Research

How the index is built, what the scores mean, and the principles that guide this research into AI's human impact on the workforce.

The Human-Centred Approach

Most AI impact tools measure workforce transformation through an economic lens: productivity gains, cost reductions, efficiency ratios, and return on automation investment. The Human Impact Index starts from a different premise. The central question is not "how much value does AI create?" but "who bears the cost of this transition, and are they receiving adequate support?"

This means the index gives weight to factors that conventional barometers typically omit: the concentration of displacement risk among lower-income workers, the adequacy of national retraining infrastructure, the gap between formal regulatory protections and real-world enforcement, and the degree to which AI adoption is happening faster than affected communities can adapt.

The tool is grounded in philosophy of technology, labour economics, and commercial real estate practice. It is designed to be useful to policy-makers, business leaders, and researchers who want a more complete picture of AI's societal impact than economic metrics alone can provide.

The Momentum Score

Each sector in each region is assigned a Momentum Score from 0 to 100. The score represents the pace and depth of AI-driven workforce transformation, not the absolute number of jobs affected. A score of 90 means transformation is happening fast, at scale, and with significant structural consequences for workers. A score of 40 means change is real but more gradual, with more time for adaptation.

Scores are calculated across five weighted dimensions:

30%
Current AI Adoption Rate

Measured penetration of AI tools in the sector, including automation of specific task types, deployment of generative AI in workflows, and robotics density where applicable.

25%
Displacement Velocity

The speed at which roles are being eliminated or fundamentally restructured, including observed redundancy rates, hiring freezes in specific functions, and forward guidance from major employers.

20%
Task Automation Exposure

The proportion of current job tasks in the sector that are technically automatable using existing or near-term AI capabilities, drawing on OECD and academic task-analysis frameworks.

15%
Investment Signal Strength

The volume and direction of capital flowing into AI tools, infrastructure, and automation within the sector, used as a leading indicator of future displacement pressure.

10%
Policy & Regulatory Friction

The degree to which regulatory frameworks, labour protections, and government policy are slowing or shaping AI adoption in ways that protect workers. Higher friction reduces the score.

Score Interpretation

Range Classification What it means
80 – 100 Extreme Transformation Rapid, structural displacement underway. Significant workforce impact already visible, with acceleration expected.
65 – 79 Very High Transformation Strong and accelerating AI adoption with material workforce consequences. Reskilling urgency is high.
50 – 64 High Transformation Significant change in progress. Role profiles shifting materially. Some displacement, more task restructuring.
35 – 49 Moderate Transformation AI adoption real but slower-paced. Workers have more time to adapt. Structural change is directional, not yet acute.
0 – 34 Early Stage AI integration beginning but limited workforce impact to date. Monitor for acceleration.
Important: scores are directional, not precise
  • Momentum scores reflect the best available evidence at time of publication. They are informed judgements, not algorithmic outputs.
  • Scores are calibrated within regions, meaning a European score of 70 reflects high transformation relative to the European context, and should not be read as identical to a North American 70.
  • Manufacturing scores across Europe carry a "Snapshot" designation where granular AI-specific displacement data is not available from official statistics. These are treated as indicative rather than fully evidenced.
  • The index does not predict absolute job loss numbers. The relationship between automation exposure and actual employment outcomes depends heavily on demand growth, policy response, and adaptation timelines.

Data Sources & Research Standards

The index prioritises primary and authoritative secondary sources. Where granular AI-specific data is not available, estimates are clearly labelled as such in the source notes of each country page. The approach follows the principle of transparent estimation over false precision: it is more valuable to acknowledge uncertainty honestly than to present a confident figure that obscures its own shakiness.

Primary sources used by region

UK ONS Labour Force Survey, CIPD workforce reports, UK government AI strategy documentation, sector-specific regulators (FCA, ICO).
North America US Bureau of Labor Statistics, Statistics Canada, McKinsey Global Institute, Stanford AI Index, sector trade associations.
Europe Eurostat, national statistics offices (Destatis, INSEE, CBS, INE, ISTAT, SCB, GUS), OECD Employment Outlook, EU AI Act implementation documentation, CNIL, BaFin, and equivalent national regulators.
Asia-Pacific Statistics Bureau Japan Labour Force Survey, Singapore Ministry of Manpower, Australian Bureau of Statistics, India PLFS/NASSCOM, NBS China, OECD APAC data where available.
Cross-regional OECD AI Policy Observatory, ILO World Employment and Social Outlook, World Economic Forum Future of Jobs reports, CBRE and JLL global market research, McKinsey Global Institute automation studies.

Regional & Country Coverage

The index covers four regions: the UK, North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific. Within Europe and APAC, individual country pages provide detailed analysis rather than treating each region as a single homogeneous market. This reflects the reality that AI adoption pace, regulatory environment, and labour market structure vary significantly even within the EU.

Country selection follows the coverage approach of major commercial real estate firms (Cushman & Wakefield, CBRE, JLL), which ensures the tool is directly relevant to the senior real estate leaders who form a core part of its intended audience. Countries covered within each region are those where these firms maintain substantive research and analytical operations.

Europe: Germany, France, Netherlands, Spain, Italy, Poland.
Asia-Pacific: Australia, Japan, Singapore, India, China.

Sector coverage across Europe and APAC follows the same nine-sector framework used for the UK and North America, with Manufacturing carried at "Snapshot" level in several European countries where official statistics do not provide the granularity needed for full analysis. This is flagged clearly on each affected page.

Update Strategy

The Human Impact Index is a living resource. AI adoption is accelerating rapidly, and a static snapshot becomes outdated quickly. The current update approach is:

Phase 1 — Current
Quarterly Manual Review

Each country and sector page is reviewed and updated quarterly using the latest available data from the primary sources listed above. Momentum scores are recalibrated when material changes in adoption pace or policy are evident.

Phase 2 — Q3 2026
Expanded Country Coverage

Planned addition of further APAC markets (South Korea, potentially Hong Kong), Nordic countries within Europe, and a more detailed Canada-specific analysis within the North America region.

Phase 3 — 2027
Community Validation

Structured input from sector practitioners, academics, and policy specialists to validate and enrich the analysis, particularly for countries where primary data availability is more constrained.

Limitations & Honest Caveats

Academic integrity requires acknowledging what this tool cannot do, as well as what it can.

  • AI-specific displacement data is not consistently published. National statistics offices categorise employment by sector and occupation, not by whether jobs have been displaced by AI specifically. The index triangulates from multiple sources, but some figures involve estimation.
  • The tool covers formal employment. In markets with large informal workforces (notably India and China), the index understates the true scale of AI impact on workers, since informal workers are largely invisible in official data and have the fewest protections.
  • Momentum scores involve judgement. The weighting of the five dimensions, and the calibration of scores within regional ranges, reflects informed analytical decisions rather than purely mechanical calculation. Reasonable analysts might weight these dimensions differently.
  • The index is not a forecast. High momentum scores indicate that transformation pressure is intense, not that a specific number of jobs will be lost. Actual employment outcomes depend on many factors beyond the scope of this tool.
  • Data currency varies by market. Some national statistics are published with a significant lag. Where the most recent available data is more than 18 months old, this is noted in the relevant source documentation.

Academic Foundation

This tool emerges from PhD research at the University of Sussex examining the ethical implications of AI systems and their relationship with human welfare, with particular focus on workforce transformation, human flourishing, and the moral responsibilities of organisations deploying AI at scale.

The analytical framework draws on philosophy of technology (Heidegger, Borgmann, Verbeek), the capability approach to human development (Sen, Nussbaum), labour economics and workforce transition theory, and ethics of automation and displacement. The commercial real estate focus reflects more than 20 years of industry experience, including a global leadership role at Cushman & Wakefield, which provides the practical grounding to assess how AI is actually being deployed in real estate operations rather than how it is theorised to be.
