Data Centres

Full Analysis
82
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China's data centre buildout is the largest in the world by growth rate, yet the human story is complex. Construction workers and low-skill operations staff face precarious employment conditions as hyperscale facilities require fewer ongoing workers per megawatt than older facilities. The government's "AI+" agenda drives investment, but wage protections and safety standards for the growing construction workforce around Inner Mongolia, Beijing, and Guangdong clusters are inadequately monitored. High-skilled AI operations roles concentrate in first-tier cities, widening the urban-rural and regional divide.

Employment Context

  • China accounts for approximately 16% of global hyperscale data centre capacity (Synergy Research, Q4 2024)
  • Greater Beijing cluster alone represents ~10% of global hyperscale capacity (Allianz Commercial, 2025)
  • Electricity capacity on course to reach 30 GW in 2025, a 30% year-on-year increase (Goldman Sachs Research, 2025)
  • Top internet firms expected to invest over $70 billion in 2026 (Goldman Sachs Research)
  • DeepSeek launch in January 2025 accelerated domestic hyperscaler investment
  • Chinese hyperscalers: Alibaba Cloud, Tencent, Baidu, ByteDance

Workforce Snapshot

  • Sector requires tens of thousands of construction workers for ongoing buildout
  • Ongoing operations roles per facility are highly automated; engineer-to-MW ratios are falling
  • Domestic chip supply chain workers are a growing employment category as China shifts from foreign chips
  • China Telecom's Inner Mongolia Data Park employs thousands in a region with few alternative employers

Roles Under Pressure

  • Traditional data centre operations technicians — automated monitoring replacing manual oversight
  • Entry-level IT support roles within facilities — AI diagnostics reducing headcount
  • Routine network monitoring — AI systems handle anomaly detection

Emerging Opportunities

  • AI infrastructure engineers and GPU cluster specialists
  • Domestic chip architecture and hardware design roles (as foreign chip dependency declines)
  • Energy and cooling systems engineers — liquid cooling at scale is a new discipline
  • AI safety and compliance roles required by CAC filing obligations

Regulatory Context

China's National Development and Reform Commission has a Data Centre Action Plan requiring facilities to improve renewable energy utilisation by 10% annually. The Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) mandates registration and security assessment for generative AI services hosted in data centres. There is no equivalent of the EU AI Act's worker protection provisions; labour rights within hyperscale construction remain governed by general labour law, which enforcement records suggest is inconsistently applied in remote cluster locations.

Logistics and Warehousing

Full Analysis
78
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China's logistics and warehousing sector is the world's most automated by volume, driven by Alibaba's Cainiao, JD Logistics, and SF Express. The human cost is concentrated among the approximately 300 million migrant workers who form the backbone of last-mile delivery and warehouse picking operations. These workers are often engaged through informal or gig arrangements, with no portable benefits and minimal retraining provision. Automation of warehouse picking and sortation is replacing entry-level roles that provide a critical first rung on the economic ladder for rural migrants. Government policy prioritises throughput; worker transition is an afterthought.

Employment Context

  • Total migrant workers: 299.73 million in 2024, up 0.7% year-on-year (NBS 2024)
  • Logistics and transport is a leading employer of migrant labour across eastern seaboard provinces
  • JD Logistics operates fully automated warehouses across 15 cities; human pickers largely replaced in sortation hubs
  • Alibaba's Cainiao system processed over 100 billion parcels in 2024 (Alibaba Group)
  • AI-powered route optimisation and autonomous delivery pilots active in Shenzhen, Shanghai, Beijing

Employment Estimate (Logistics)

  • Estimated 20–25 million workers in logistics and warehousing (estimate based on sector's share of urban employment and migrant worker surveys; no single NBS sub-sector figure available)
  • Last-mile delivery alone employs an estimated 7–8 million couriers (industry estimates, 2024)
  • Automated sortation centres: picking robot penetration exceeds 60% in JD and Cainiao major hubs

Roles Under Pressure

  • Warehouse pickers and sorters — robotic arms and AMR fleets replacing manual picking in tier-1 hubs
  • Manual sortation workers — automated conveyor and vision systems
  • Long-haul truck drivers — autonomous and semi-autonomous freight pilots expanding
  • Inventory clerks — AI-driven RFID and vision systems replace manual counting

Emerging Opportunities

  • Robot fleet supervisors and AMR maintenance technicians
  • Last-mile drone operations coordinators
  • AI logistics planning and route optimisation analysts
  • Cold-chain AI systems technicians (growing food e-commerce segment)

Regulatory Context

China's New Generation AI Development Plan (updated 2024) designates logistics as a priority sector for AI integration but contains no binding worker protection provisions. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security operates reskilling fund schemes, but coverage of gig and informal logistics workers is structurally excluded from most provisions. Algorithmic management of delivery workers is governed only by a 2021 guidance document on rider platform obligations, which is inadequately enforced.

Office Traditional

Full Analysis
65
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China's traditional office workforce — accountants, administrative staff, clerks, data entry workers, and junior analysts — faces the most direct exposure to generative AI displacement in the near term. A Peking University study of millions of job postings from 2018 to 2024 found a clear decline in vacancies for occupations with high AI exposure, particularly in text and data processing roles. The researchers concluded that "some of the job positions in such occupations are being replaced by AI, so that firms could cut new hiring." The 12.7 million university graduates entering the market in 2025 face an environment where AI is already narrowing the entry points that previous generations used to build careers.

Roles Under Pressure

  • Accountants and bookkeepers — AI coding and reconciliation tools from platforms like Alibaba Cloud
  • Editors and content reviewers — large language models handling first-pass drafting
  • Junior programmers — code generation reducing new-hire demand at Baidu, ByteDance, and tech firms
  • Administrative assistants — generative AI scheduling and documentation tools
  • Data entry and processing clerks — OCR and AI extraction eliminating manual input

Emerging Opportunities

  • AI governance and ethics compliance officers (CAC filing and algorithm audit requirements)
  • Prompt engineering and AI workflow designers
  • Human-in-the-loop oversight roles for regulated AI outputs
  • Cross-border business liaisons as Chinese firms expand internationally

Regulatory Context

The Interim Measures for Generative AI Services (2023) require AI outputs to meet content standards, creating demand for human reviewers. However, the regulatory framework does not address the displacement of traditional office roles, nor does it require employers to provide transition support for workers whose tasks are automated. Baidu reportedly cut up to 40% of some departments in late 2024 as AI tools reduced headcount needs.

Office Tech and Creative

Full Analysis
80
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China's tech sector is simultaneously the primary beneficiary of AI investment and the sector generating the most acute near-term displacement among knowledge workers. AI-related job openings surged 543% year-on-year between January and October 2025 (Maimai recruitment platform), while vacancies for conventional programming and data-processing roles declined. This polarisation is sharpest for younger workers: 12.7 million university graduates in 2025, many holding computer science or related degrees, enter a market where the entry-level roles that structured previous career paths are contracting rapidly. The pay premium for AI-native talent (algorithm engineers earning 18% more than general counterparts) widens inequality within the tech workforce.

Roles Under Pressure

  • General software developers — code-generation AI compressing team sizes
  • UX/UI designers without AI tool proficiency — generative design tools devalue mid-tier skills
  • Content creators and copywriters — domestic LLMs (Doubao, Kimi, DeepSeek) replacing output
  • Junior data analysts — automated insight generation from BI platforms

Emerging Opportunities

  • Large model algorithm engineers — top of demand charts on all major platforms
  • AI product managers — earning 20%+ above traditional counterparts
  • AI video and multimodal content specialists — growing international revenue stream
  • Domestic AI chip architects — critical as China reduces foreign chip dependence
  • AI safety and alignment researchers — CAC filing obligations create institutional demand

Retail

Full Analysis
72
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

Chinese retail has undergone one of the most aggressive AI-driven transformations in the world. Alibaba's Freshippo and JD's 7FRESH pioneered cashierless grocery formats; live-stream commerce employs millions but also creates a highly volatile, algorithmically managed gig workforce. The workers bearing the greatest cost of this transition are the tens of millions of low-skill retail assistants, many of whom are older workers or women from non-coastal regions who have limited pathways into AI-adjacent roles. The algorithmic management of live-stream workers — who face penalty systems, ranking pressures, and performance data exposure — represents a form of intensification that traditional labour protections were not designed to address.

Roles Under Pressure

  • Cashiers and checkout staff — cashierless stores and mobile payment saturation
  • Inventory and stock management workers — RFID and vision AI systems
  • Customer service agents — AI chatbots handling first-line queries on Taobao, JD.com
  • Traditional shop floor assistants — foot-traffic decline in physical retail

Emerging Opportunities

  • Live-stream commerce hosts and AI-assisted selling coordinators
  • Personalisation data analysts for e-commerce platforms
  • Smart store experience designers
  • AI-assisted visual merchandising specialists

Commercial Real Estate Services

Full Analysis
62
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China's commercial real estate services sector faces a double pressure: a structural correction in the property market following the 2021 developer debt crisis, and the concurrent introduction of AI tools that compress the advisory and analytical workforce. Firms such as CBRE, JLL, and Cushman and Wakefield operate in China but face a domestically depressed market that limits expansion. The workers most exposed are junior analysts and property management staff whose routine functions — valuation modelling, lease abstraction, facilities reporting — are already being replaced by AI platforms. The domestic market's prolonged weakness means there is no compensation through growth.

Roles Under Pressure

  • Junior CRE analysts — AI valuation and market research tools
  • Property management admin staff — automated workflow and reporting
  • Facilities management coordinators — IoT and AI-driven building management systems

Emerging Opportunities

  • Data centre real estate specialists — hyperscale demand creates new sub-market
  • ESG and sustainability analysts for commercial portfolios
  • Smart building systems integrators

Professional Services

Full Analysis
68
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China's professional services sector — encompassing law, consulting, accountancy, and research — faces growing AI integration at the entry and mid levels. The Peking University study found that "accountants, editors, and programmers" are among the highest AI-exposed knowledge occupations in China. The structural challenge is that these professions represent the aspirational destination for the millions of annual university graduates, many of whom now face a narrowing market. Law firms are using AI for contract review; Big Four accounting firms are deploying AI for audit analytics; consulting firms are replacing junior research roles with AI-assisted analysis. The safety net for displaced professional workers — retraining access, unemployment insurance — is designed for manufacturing workers and is poorly suited to knowledge-worker transitions.

Roles Under Pressure

  • Junior lawyers and legal assistants — AI contract review and due diligence tools
  • Junior auditors and accountants — AI-driven audit analytics and reconciliation
  • Research analysts — generative AI synthesising reports
  • Translators — large multilingual models replacing routine translation work

Emerging Opportunities

  • AI governance consultants serving firms required to comply with CAC regulations
  • Senior client-facing advisory roles where relationships and judgement remain central
  • AI audit and model risk validation specialists
  • IP attorneys specialising in AI-generated content (a growing case load since 2023 Beijing Internet Court rulings)

Manufacturing

Full Analysis
76
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

China installs one in every two industrial robots globally, and has been the world's leading robotics market since 2013. Yet the human impact is more nuanced than displacement narratives suggest. A 2025 survey of 2,400 manufacturing workers in southern China found that only 2% had been reassigned or laid off due to technological changes in the past two years — the primary automation driver to date has been demographic necessity, as young workers shun factory work and China's workforce shrinks. However, the same workers believe nearly 50% of their jobs could be automated in the next five to ten years. The workers least equipped to navigate this transition are those in mid-skill assembly roles in the Pearl River and Yangtze River Delta regions — older, often migrant workers with no higher education and limited access to state reskilling programmes.

Employment Context

  • Industrial sector employs approximately 29% of the workforce — an estimated 213 million people (NBS 2023 breakdown; 2024 figures not separately published in English)
  • Manufacturing accounts for the majority of industrial employment (estimate: 90–100 million workers; NBS sub-sector breakdowns are not fully available in English-language sources; treat as informed estimate)
  • High-tech manufacturing value added grew 8.9% in 2024 (NBS)
  • New energy vehicle output: 13.168 million units in 2024, up 38.7% (NBS)
  • Service robots: 10.519 million units produced in 2024, up 15.6% (NBS)

Roles Under Pressure

  • Assembly line workers in consumer electronics — robotic arms penetration exceeds 70% in leading facilities
  • Quality inspection workers — AI vision systems now outperform human inspection in defect detection
  • Textile and garment workers — labour-intensive sectors facing automation and nearshoring
  • Routine CNC machine operators — AI-driven precision machining reduces operator involvement

Emerging Opportunities

  • Robotics maintenance and calibration technicians
  • AI-driven quality systems engineers
  • New energy vehicle battery assembly specialists
  • Industrial AI trainers and digital twin operators
  • Advanced semiconductor process engineers

Financial Services

Full Analysis
74
Momentum

Human Impact Assessment

Chinese financial institutions were early and aggressive adopters of AI — Ant Group, Ping An, and state banks have deployed AI in credit scoring, fraud detection, and customer service at enormous scale. The human impact falls hardest on branch staff and back-office processors. Bank branch networks have contracted significantly since 2020, with AI-driven mobile banking reducing the need for human transaction processing. Workers in smaller regional banks and third-tier cities lack the digital literacy or geographic mobility to transition into AI-adjacent roles. The government's priority is financial stability and technological leadership; displaced bank workers receive no sector-specific transition support.

Roles Under Pressure

  • Bank tellers and branch cashiers — mobile banking penetration exceeds 90% among urban under-45s
  • Loan officers (routine applications) — AI credit scoring replacing manual review for SME lending
  • Insurance claims processors — Ping An and others using AI for automated claims assessment
  • Compliance monitoring staff — RegTech and AI surveillance replacing manual transaction review
  • Customer service agents — AI chatbots handling the vast majority of first-contact queries

Emerging Opportunities

  • AI risk model validators and explainability specialists
  • Wealth management advisers serving growing middle class
  • Financial data scientists and quantitative analysts
  • Digital RMB implementation specialists
  • Fintech product developers working on cross-border payment rails

Regulatory Context

China's financial AI regulation is sector-specific and distributed across the People's Bank of China, CBIRC, and CSRC. The CAC's Generative AI Measures apply to customer-facing AI tools. Algorithmic credit scoring is governed by personal information protection rules (PIPL, 2021), but mandatory explainability requirements for automated decisions affecting individuals are inconsistently enforced. There is no requirement for financial institutions to notify workers in advance of role automation, nor to fund retraining. China's digital yuan (e-CNY) programme is accelerating the replacement of cash transaction roles.

Policy Gaps: What the Frameworks Do Not Address

No Mandatory Transition Support

China's AI regulatory framework is focused on content governance and national security, not on worker outcomes. There is no equivalent of a European social safety net obligation linked to AI-driven displacement. Reskilling schemes exist but are not designed for the informal and migrant labour that bears the heaviest burden.

Migrant Worker Structural Exclusion

China's hukou household registration system means that the 300 million migrant workers are often ineligible for urban social insurance, unemployment benefits, and training programmes in the cities where they work. AI displacement is therefore most damaging precisely for the workers with the fewest institutional protections.

Graduate Market Crisis Unaddressed

With 12.7 million university graduates entering the market in 2025, and AI narrowing entry-level knowledge work at precisely the career entry points graduates depend on, there is a structural collision between supply and demand that government policy has not yet adequately diagnosed, let alone addressed.

Algorithmic Management Without Rights

Tens of millions of Chinese gig workers — delivery riders, live-stream hosts, warehouse pickers — are managed by algorithmic systems. The 2021 rider platform guidance is inadequately enforced. Workers have no right to explanation, no appeal mechanism, and no data access regarding how their algorithmic score is calculated.

No Independent Labour Voice

The absence of independent trade unions in China means there is no organised worker voice to negotiate AI transition terms. The All-China Federation of Trade Unions operates within the political system and has not taken a substantive role in AI workforce policy. Workers cannot collectively bargain against automation.

Innovation Bias in Regulation

China's AI regulatory posture explicitly prioritises innovation and national competitiveness. The CAC's Generative AI Measures encourage development while imposing content obligations. Worker impact is outside the regulatory frame. This is a policy gap that may generate significant social instability as displacement accelerates toward the late 2020s.
