Current Landscape

Healthcare is the largest employer in the United States, with approximately 16 million people working across hospitals, physician practices, long-term care facilities, home health agencies, insurance companies, pharmaceutical manufacturers, medical device firms, and the vast administrative infrastructure that the US system's complexity requires. It is also, in important respects, the sector whose disruption dynamic most defies simple characterisation. Unlike most sectors where the headline story is net job loss, healthcare presents a genuinely bifurcated picture: significant administrative role compression through AI automation running simultaneously with acute and growing shortages of clinical workers that no amount of AI deployment can quickly resolve.

The United States spends approximately $4.5 trillion annually on healthcare, more than 17% of GDP and more per capita than any comparable nation by a substantial margin. A remarkable proportion of that expenditure, estimated at 25-35% by various health economics analyses, goes to administrative costs: billing and coding, prior authorisation, claims processing, regulatory compliance, credentialling, scheduling, and the enormous overhead of navigating a system built around thousands of distinct payers, plans, and reimbursement models. This administrative burden is the primary target of healthcare AI deployment, and its reduction, while economically justified and in some respects clinically beneficial by freeing clinical time, represents a significant employment displacement concentrated in specific categories of healthcare worker.

The clinical side presents a near-opposite picture. The Association of American Medical Colleges projects a shortage of up to 86,000 physicians by 2036. The nursing workforce is under sustained pressure from retirements, burnout-driven attrition, and inadequate pipeline growth. Allied health professions including radiologic technologists, respiratory therapists, and medical laboratory scientists face shortages that AI tools, at best, partially mitigate without resolving. The AI disruption of healthcare is therefore not a single story of displacement but a complex reallocation, one where the workers at greatest risk and the workers in greatest demand are, with limited exceptions, entirely different populations.

Healthcare is one of the few sectors in this tool where AI deployment is producing genuinely positive patient outcomes in parallel with its workforce consequences. AI diagnostic tools including Google's DeepMind for eye disease detection, PathAI for pathology, and Aidoc for radiology triage are demonstrably improving diagnostic accuracy, reducing missed diagnoses, and accelerating time-to-treatment in conditions where speed is clinically critical. The human impact analysis must hold both realities simultaneously: AI is reducing some categories of healthcare employment while also saving lives, and the policy challenge is ensuring that the displaced workers are treated as a central concern rather than acceptable collateral of a beneficial technological transition.

The Administrative Burden: Healthcare's Largest Automation Target

The administrative complexity of the US healthcare system is, by international comparison, extraordinary. The United States spent an estimated $812 billion on healthcare administration in 2019, approximately 34% of total healthcare spending, compared to roughly 12% in Canada's single-payer provinces. This administrative overhead, generated by the fragmentation of the US payer system, its prior authorisation requirements, its complex billing and coding infrastructure, and its credentialling and compliance obligations, employs millions of people in roles that in countries with simpler health systems either do not exist or require far fewer workers.

Medical Billing, Coding, and Revenue Cycle

Medical coding, the translation of clinical events into the ICD-10 and CPT code sets used for billing, employs approximately 230,000 dedicated coders in the United States, plus substantial additional coding work performed by clinical staff and outsourced vendors. AI coding tools, including those from companies such as Optum, Change Healthcare (now part of UnitedHealth), Waystar, and a range of specialist vendors, are now capable of performing first-pass coding on the majority of routine clinical encounters with accuracy rates comparable to experienced human coders. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects a 9% decline in medical records and health information technologist employment over the decade to 2032, but industry analysts believe this understates the AI-driven compression that is now accelerating.

Prior authorisation, the process by which insurers require providers to obtain approval before delivering certain treatments or medications, employs tens of thousands of staff at both insurance companies and provider organisations to manage the volume of requests, denials, and appeals. AI systems that can automatically populate prior auth requests, anticipate denial risk, and initiate appeals are reducing the human labour required per transaction materially. Several major insurers have deployed AI to process prior auth requests, a deployment that has attracted significant regulatory and congressional attention due to concerns that AI denial rates are higher than appropriate clinical standards would produce.

Clinical Documentation and Ambient AI

Clinical documentation burden is among the most significant contributors to physician burnout in the United States. The average physician spends approximately two hours on administrative tasks, primarily electronic health record documentation, for every one hour of direct patient contact. Ambient AI documentation tools, including Nuance DAX (Microsoft), Suki, Abridge, and tools built into Epic's EHR system, can listen to clinical conversations and automatically generate structured clinical notes, referral letters, and after-visit summaries without requiring physician dictation or manual entry.

The clinical employment consequence of ambient AI documentation is primarily positive in the short term: it reduces physician and nurse documentation burden, potentially improving retention and reducing burnout-driven attrition from the clinical workforce. The longer-term consequence is more complex. If ambient AI substantially reduces the administrative support required for documentation workflows, roles including medical scribes, transcriptionists, and some medical assistant functions will be compressed. Medical scribes, who accompany physicians to provide real-time documentation support, are already seeing their employment model challenged by ambient AI tools that provide equivalent documentation support without a dedicated human scribe.

Epic Systems, the dominant EHR vendor with approximately 35% of US hospital market share and 39% of physician office market share, has embedded AI throughout its platform including AI-generated clinical notes, AI-assisted medication ordering, and AI-flagged care gaps. The 274 million patients whose records are in Epic systems provide an extraordinary training dataset for clinical AI, and Epic's market position means that the AI tools it ships have immediate scale that specialist vendors cannot match.

UnitedHealth Group's Optum division deployed an AI model to make prior authorisation decisions for post-acute care, resulting in a denial rate that a class action lawsuit alleged far exceeded what clinical criteria justified. ProPublica's 2023 investigation documented specific cases in which the AI model denied coverage for rehabilitation stays based on statistical patterns in training data rather than individual patient clinical assessment. The lawsuit and investigation illustrate that AI in healthcare administrative processes is not a neutral efficiency tool: when it makes coverage decisions that affect patient access to care, its errors have human health consequences that billing automation errors in other sectors do not. The human impact of healthcare AI is inseparable from patient outcomes.

Clinical AI: Augmenting the Workforce in Shortage

The clinical application of AI in healthcare represents a genuinely different disruption dynamic from the administrative story. Where administrative AI primarily threatens employment of lower-and-middle-income workers in billing and coding roles, clinical AI is operating in a context of severe workforce shortage, where the primary risk is not displacement of existing workers but the inadequate deployment of AI tools that could extend the reach of a clinical workforce that is already insufficient to meet demand.

Radiology is the clinical specialism that has attracted the most AI development attention and the most extensive deployment experience. FDA-cleared AI algorithms can now analyse chest X-rays for pneumonia, pulmonary nodules, and pneumothorax; mammograms for breast cancer; retinal images for diabetic retinopathy; and CT scans for stroke and pulmonary embolism, with accuracy in controlled studies that meets or exceeds average radiologist performance for specific finding categories. More than 700 AI medical devices have received FDA clearance as of 2025, the majority in radiology.

The employment consequence for radiologists is not straightforward displacement. The radiology workforce in the United States is approximately 34,000 radiologists, facing its own shortage driven by training pipeline constraints and growing imaging volume. AI tools are functioning primarily as triage and prioritisation systems that ensure urgent findings are flagged immediately, reduce the cognitive burden on radiologists reviewing high-volume routine studies, and reduce the rate of missed incidental findings. The net effect is more radiologist capacity per person rather than fewer radiologists required, at least in the current shortage context. If imaging volume continues to grow and the radiologist shortage persists, AI may prevent a staffing crisis rather than causing a displacement one.

Pathology presents a similar trajectory. AI digital pathology systems from companies including PathAI and Paige.AI can analyse whole-slide images for cancer grade, tumour margin assessment, and biomarker status with accuracy competitive with pathologist review. The pathologist shortage, particularly for subspecialty oncology pathology in community and rural settings, means that AI tools extending the reach of available pathologists are addressing access failures rather than creating employment ones. The longer-term dynamic, as AI pathology accuracy continues to improve and training volume accumulates, is more uncertain.

AI Drug Discovery: Accelerating the Possible

The pharmaceutical industry's adoption of AI in drug discovery represents the most significant transformation in the life sciences research process in decades. Traditional drug discovery, moving from target identification through hit finding, lead optimisation, preclinical development, and clinical trials, is a process measured in years and costing billions of dollars per successful drug. AI tools are compressing several of the most time-consuming stages, with material implications for both the speed of therapeutic development and the workforce that performs it.

Isomorphic Labs, DeepMind's life sciences subsidiary, published AlphaFold2 in 2021, solving the protein structure prediction problem that had challenged structural biology for fifty years. AlphaFold's database of predicted protein structures, now freely available and covering essentially all known proteins, has removed a bottleneck from early-stage drug discovery that previously required years of crystallography or cryo-EM work. The research teams whose primary function was structural determination face a changed landscape in which AI has made their core technical contribution less scarce.

AI-driven molecular design, using tools such as Schrödinger's computational chemistry platform, Recursion Pharmaceuticals' phenomics approach, and Insilico Medicine's generative chemistry, is accelerating the identification and optimisation of drug candidates in ways that reduce the number of synthesis-and-test cycles required. A process that previously required synthesising and testing hundreds of compounds to identify promising leads can now be substantially conducted in silico, with synthesis and testing focused on a smaller number of AI-selected candidates with higher predicted activity.

The net workforce implication for pharmaceutical research is a compression of some categories of medicinal chemistry and early-stage biology roles as AI reduces the iteration required to reach clinical candidates, alongside growth in computational chemistry, machine learning, and data science roles. The pharmaceutical companies managing this transition most deliberately, including Eli Lilly, AstraZeneca, and Novartis, are investing in retraining programmes for their experimental research scientists rather than simply reducing headcount as AI tools reduce the labour content of their traditional workflows.

The Nursing Crisis: When Automation Cannot Solve the Core Problem

The United States faces a nursing shortage of approximately 450,000 nurses by 2025 according to the American Nurses Association, a figure projected to grow to 1.1 million by 2030 if current attrition and training pipeline trends continue. This shortage exists alongside the AI disruption of healthcare's administrative workforce, creating a paradox that simple disruption narratives cannot accommodate: the healthcare sector is simultaneously automating tens of thousands of administrative roles and desperately short of the clinical workers it cannot replace with automation.

Why AI Cannot Substitute for Clinical Care

The nursing role encompasses tasks that span a spectrum from those that AI tools can genuinely support to those that are irreducibly human. Medication administration, wound care, patient mobility assistance, intravenous line management, and the physical assessment of deteriorating patients require trained clinical judgment and physical presence that no current or near-term AI system can substitute. Beyond the technical tasks, nursing involves emotional presence, patient advocacy, communication, and the kind of situational awareness of a patient's overall condition that experienced nurses develop over years of practice and that AI systems, however sophisticated their pattern recognition, cannot replicate in the richly contextual, rapidly changing environment of acute clinical care.

AI tools are being deployed to support nurses, including AI early warning systems that flag deteriorating patients before vital signs reach crisis thresholds, medication reconciliation tools that flag potential interactions, and natural language interfaces to EHR systems that reduce documentation time. These tools genuinely improve nursing workflow and reduce preventable errors. They do not reduce the number of nurses needed; in many cases they reveal previously undetected clinical complexity that requires more nursing time, not less.

Burnout, Pipeline, and the Staffing Agency Premium

The nursing shortage is substantially a retention crisis driven by burnout, and it was acute before the COVID-19 pandemic and dramatically worsened by it. Hospital nurses report leaving bedside care for travel nursing, agency work, or other careers in large numbers, motivated by workload intensity, inadequate staffing ratios, and the moral injury of providing care under conditions they consider inadequate. The consequences for healthcare systems are severe: hospitals paying travel nurse agencies $150-250 per hour for nurses they need to fill roster gaps, compared to $40-65 per hour for their own staff nurses, creating an ongoing financial crisis that is compounding the systemic investment challenges in clinical workforce development.

Medical assistant roles, which support clinical workflows in physician offices and ambulatory care settings, are facing a dual pressure: automation of some of their administrative functions while the clinical support tasks they perform cannot be automated and are growing in demand. Medical assistants are a significant workforce, approximately 760,000 in the US, disproportionately female and minority, and their role in ambulatory care is growing as care shifts from inpatient to outpatient settings. AI tools that reduce the administrative component of their role create both efficiency and a potential employment risk if the administrative tasks that were eliminated were what justified their full-time employment.

Job Transformation Dynamics

Roles Under Significant Pressure
  • Medical Coders and Billing Specialists
    AI medical coding tools performing first-pass coding on routine encounters with 85-90% accuracy, requiring human review rather than human generation. BLS projects 9% decline over ten years; industry analysts tracking AI deployment expect acceleration to 20-30% decline in dedicated coding roles by 2030. Disproportionately female workforce with high remote-work prevalence, meaning displacement occurs without geographic visibility in healthcare facility employment statistics.
  • Medical Transcriptionists
    AI voice recognition and ambient documentation tools have already substantially displaced medical transcriptionist employment over the past decade. The approximately 55,000 remaining transcriptionists in the US are working primarily in specialised or quality-assurance functions that AI tools have not yet fully automated. Further decline of 35-45% by 2030 as ambient AI documentation becomes the standard of care in most clinical environments.
  • Health Insurance Claims Processors
    Claims processing, eligibility verification, and prior authorisation management at health insurers are among the most directly automatable administrative functions in the healthcare system. AI claims adjudication systems at major insurers are processing the majority of routine claims without human review. The insurance administrative workforce is expected to decline by 15-25% by 2030, with the reductions concentrated in claims processing, data entry, and routine customer service roles.
  • Medical Scribes
    Medical scribes, who provide real-time documentation support to physicians during clinical encounters, number approximately 100,000 in the US and have been growing rapidly as a profession driven by physician documentation burden. Ambient AI documentation tools, including Nuance DAX and Abridge, are providing equivalent documentation support without a dedicated human scribe, at lower cost and without the scheduling complexity. Estimated 40-55% decline in dedicated medical scribe roles by 2030 as ambient AI deployment reaches mainstream adoption.
  • Early-Stage Pharmaceutical Research Associates
    Research associates performing high-throughput screening, routine synthesis, and early-stage data analysis in pharmaceutical R&D face compression as AI tools reduce the number of experimental iterations required to identify promising drug candidates. The research associate role as a standalone position is under pressure, with pharmaceutical companies increasingly seeking hybrid profiles combining wet laboratory skills with computational capability to work alongside AI discovery platforms.
Emerging Roles and Growing Opportunities
  • Clinical AI Implementation and Validation Specialists
    Deploying, validating, and monitoring AI clinical decision support tools requires professionals who combine clinical domain knowledge with understanding of AI system behaviour, bias, and failure modes. Health systems deploying AI diagnostic tools need staff who can validate performance across patient populations, monitor for drift, and manage the clinician change management required for adoption. This is a genuinely new role category with strong demand and limited supply.
  • Nurse Practitioners and Physician Assistants
    Advanced practice providers are among the clearest growth categories in US healthcare, driven by physician shortages, scope of practice expansion in most states, and the demonstrated capacity of NPs and PAs to deliver high-quality primary and specialty care. AI tools that support diagnostic decision-making and clinical documentation amplify the capacity of advanced practice providers further, making them even more effective at managing complex patient panels. Projected growth of 45-55% in NP and PA employment by 2033 according to BLS.
  • Computational Biologists and AI Drug Discovery Scientists
    The pharmaceutical industry's AI drug discovery investments are creating sustained demand for scientists who can bridge biological domain knowledge with machine learning capability. Computational biologists, cheminformaticians, and AI-trained medicinal chemists command significant compensation premiums over experimental-only equivalents and are being recruited aggressively by both pharmaceutical companies and AI drug discovery startups. Academic training programmes in these hybrid disciplines are growing but remain insufficient to meet demand.
  • Home Health Aides and Personal Care Workers
    The ageing US population is driving extraordinary demand growth for home health aides and personal care workers, the roles that support older adults and people with disabilities in living independently. AI monitoring and remote sensing tools extend the reach of these workers, enabling them to support more clients with better safety outcomes. But the core work of personal care remains irreducibly human, and the projected shortage of home health workers, estimated at 3.2 million positions by 2040, dwarfs any plausible automation offset. These roles are among the lowest compensated in healthcare, a social policy failure as much as a market outcome.
  • Health Data Analysts and Population Health Specialists
    The shift from fee-for-service to value-based care payment models creates demand for analysts who can identify high-risk patients, design preventive interventions, and measure outcomes across populations. AI tools that process large claims, EHR, and social determinants datasets require human analysts to translate insights into care management programmes and health system strategy. Population health management is one of the genuine growth areas in healthcare employment that AI enables rather than threatens.
  • Clinical Nurses and Allied Health Professionals (Transforming)
    The transformation of clinical nursing and allied health roles through AI is primarily one of tool augmentation rather than displacement. Nurses equipped with AI early warning systems, AI medication reconciliation, and ambient documentation are more effective at the core clinical work that remains irreducibly theirs. The challenge is ensuring that the administrative burden reduction delivered by AI tools translates into improved working conditions and retained staff rather than simply being absorbed as additional patient load without additional compensation or staffing.

Human Impact Considerations

The Demographics of Healthcare Administrative Displacement

Healthcare administrative roles, the category most directly threatened by AI automation, are disproportionately held by women, at approximately 74% female across billing, coding, and claims processing roles, and include high concentrations of workers from racial minority groups, particularly in medical assistant and support roles. These are mid-wage positions, typically earning $35,000-$55,000 annually, that represent accessible employment pathways for workers without four-year degrees in communities where healthcare is one of the more stable large employers. The displacement of these roles by AI automation is not a white-collar knowledge worker story; it is a working-class employment story whose consequences fall on people with limited financial buffers and limited pathways to alternative employment of comparable compensation and stability.

The Patient Safety Dimension of AI Deployment Speed

The deployment of AI in clinical decision support raises patient safety considerations that have no direct parallel in other sectors covered by this tool. When AI coding software makes an error, a billing dispute follows; when AI diagnostic software misses a cancer, a patient may die. The FDA's regulatory framework for AI medical devices, including the evolving guidance on predetermined change control plans that allow AI algorithms to update without full re-review, is attempting to balance innovation speed against safety assurance in ways that have direct human consequences beyond the workforce. The workers whose expertise is being partially automated, including radiologists, pathologists, and clinical specialists, are also the professionals whose judgment serves as the human oversight layer for AI systems whose failures can be irreversible. Reducing this workforce too aggressively, in the name of efficiency, removes the safety net for AI systems that are not yet reliable enough to operate without it.

Equity in AI Health Tool Deployment and Performance

Multiple studies have documented that AI health diagnostic tools perform less accurately on patients from racial minority groups, particularly Black patients, when those tools were trained predominantly on datasets that underrepresented those populations. Dermatology AI tools trained primarily on images of lighter skin tones perform less accurately on darker skin tones. Pulse oximetry, which underpins many AI monitoring systems, has known accuracy limitations for patients with darker skin pigmentation, a limitation that contributed to missed hypoxia in COVID-19 patients. The deployment of AI tools that perform inequitably is not simply an employment concern; it is a patient safety and health equity concern that demands specific attention in both regulatory frameworks and deployment practice. The communities whose workers are being displaced from healthcare administrative roles are often the same communities whose patients face the highest risk of being inadequately served by AI clinical tools trained on data that underrepresented them.

Rural Healthcare and the AI Access Divide

The healthcare AI access divide is acute between urban academic medical centres with sophisticated IT infrastructure and the rural and safety-net providers that serve the communities with the highest health needs. Critical access hospitals with fewer than 25 beds, federally qualified health centres serving low-income urban populations, and rural primary care practices operating on thin margins do not have the capital, IT capability, or technical staff to deploy sophisticated AI tools, even when those tools would genuinely improve patient outcomes. The result is a likely widening of the already significant quality gap between care available in well-resourced urban centres and care available in rural and low-income communities, mediated by differential AI adoption. Policy investment in ensuring that AI health tools reach underserved providers is a health equity imperative as much as a technology deployment question.

The Moral Injury of Caring Work Performed Under Inadequate Conditions

Clinical healthcare workers, particularly nurses and allied health professionals, frequently describe their distress not primarily in terms of physical workload but in terms of moral injury: the distress that arises from being unable to provide the standard of care they believe their patients deserve, because staffing levels, time pressure, and administrative burden prevent it. AI tools that reduce administrative burden and improve the quality of clinical information available at the point of care have genuine potential to reduce moral injury in clinical work. But this potential is realised only if the efficiency gains from AI deployment are invested in improved staffing ratios and clinical conditions rather than extracted as margin by health system operators. The human impact of healthcare AI depends critically on whether the productivity gains it delivers are shared with the workers whose labour generates them, or appropriated entirely as financial returns for investors and executives.

Future Prospects: 2025-2030

Employment Scenario Projections 2024-2030 (US Healthcare, thousands)

Healthcare employment projections carry the unusual characteristic of having strong underlying growth drivers, the ageing population, growing chronic disease burden, and expanding coverage, that are likely to sustain overall employment levels even as AI automation displaces specific role categories. The net employment trajectory is therefore less negative than most other sectors in this analysis, but this aggregate stability masks the significant compositional disruption occurring within the sector.

A conservative scenario, in which AI administrative automation is moderated by regulatory caution, workforce advocacy, and the complexity of healthcare IT integration, and in which the clinical workforce shortage is partially addressed by training pipeline expansion and immigration, projects healthcare employment growing to approximately 17.5 million by 2030. This scenario implies net job growth of approximately 1.5 million, with administrative role declines more than offset by clinical and population health growth.

A base scenario, reflecting current AI deployment trajectories and workforce trends, projects healthcare employment growing to approximately 16.5-17 million by 2030, implying net growth of 500,000 to 1 million positions. Under this scenario, administrative roles decline by 600,000-800,000 while clinical, data science, and AI implementation roles grow by 1.1-1.8 million. The composition shift is significant even if the headline is net positive: the workers displaced from administrative roles are largely not the workers who fill the clinical and technical roles that grow.

A disruptive scenario, in which AI clinical decision support tools demonstrate sufficient reliability to support expanded scope of practice for AI-augmented non-physician clinicians and in which administrative AI automation proceeds at the maximum technically feasible pace, could see administrative employment decline by 1.2-1.5 million while overall healthcare employment grows modestly. The distributional consequences of this scenario are more acute even than the aggregate figures imply.

Policy Recommendations

Prior Authorisation Reform and AI Accountability

Federal legislation should establish minimum standards for prior authorisation processes that include mandatory human clinical review of AI-generated denial decisions for treatments above defined cost or complexity thresholds, time limits on prior authorisation response, transparency requirements for the criteria and algorithms used in automated review, and appeal rights that include access to the clinical data and algorithmic basis for denials. The CMS Prior Authorization Rule of 2024 is a step toward this framework; it should be extended to commercial insurers and strengthened with enforceable penalties for AI denial systems that demonstrably exceed clinically appropriate denial rates.

Healthcare Administrative Worker Transition Support

The federal government should establish a Healthcare Workforce Transition programme providing income support, retraining grants, and career counselling specifically for healthcare administrative workers displaced by AI automation. Community colleges in healthcare-employment-heavy markets should receive dedicated funding for curricula bridging medical coding and billing experience toward clinical documentation support, health data analysis, population health coordination, and AI tool implementation roles. The healthcare system benefits economically from administrative AI deployment; a small levy on health system AI deployment savings should fund the transition support for the workers displaced by those savings.

AI Health Tool Equity and Performance Standards

FDA should require that AI medical device submissions include performance data stratified by patient race, ethnicity, sex, age, and socioeconomic status, and should set minimum performance equity thresholds as conditions of clearance. Post-market surveillance requirements should mandate ongoing performance monitoring across patient subgroups and mandatory reporting of performance disparities above defined thresholds. Health systems deploying AI diagnostic tools should be required to conduct equity audits of AI performance in their specific patient populations before full deployment, with results reportable to state health departments and CMS. AI tools that demonstrably underperform for specific patient populations should be subject to mandatory remediation or restricted deployment conditions.

Clinical Workforce Pipeline Investment

The nursing and allied health workforce shortage is not addressable through AI deployment alone and requires sustained investment in clinical training pipeline expansion: nursing school capacity expansion, clinical training site development, immigrant clinician credential recognition reform, and student loan forgiveness for clinical health workers who commit to shortage area practice. Congress should substantially increase funding for the Health Resources and Services Administration workforce development programmes and for Title VIII nursing workforce grants, the primary federal investments in nursing education that have been chronically underfunded relative to the stated national interest in healthcare workforce adequacy.

Rural Healthcare AI Access Investment

Federal investment in AI health tool access for rural and safety-net providers should be a specific programme priority, recognising that the commercial AI health market will serve well-resourced urban academic centres before rural critical access hospitals and federally qualified health centres. The USDA Rural Health programme, HRSA rural health grants, and CMS innovation models should all include AI adoption support components, including IT infrastructure grants, implementation technical assistance, and shared AI tool licensing that allows small providers to access tools they could not afford to procure individually. The health equity consequences of differential AI access in healthcare are severe enough to warrant a considered public investment response.

Minimum Staffing Standards and AI Efficiency Sharing

CMS should establish and enforce minimum nurse-to-patient staffing ratios in Medicare and Medicaid-participating facilities, a standard that California has implemented at state level with documented patient safety benefits. These standards should include provisions ensuring that efficiency gains from AI tool deployment translate into improved staffing ratios or working conditions rather than simply reduced labour costs, creating an accountability mechanism that aligns the financial benefits of healthcare AI with the clinical and worker welfare outcomes that justify its deployment. Facilities that demonstrate AI-enabled efficiency gains should be required to demonstrate equivalent improvements in clinical staffing ratios or worker compensation as a condition of sustained Medicare and Medicaid participation at current reimbursement rates.
