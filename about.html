About the Project

A human-centred lens on AI and work

The AI Human Impact Index exists because the dominant conversation about AI in commercial real estate focuses on productivity, efficiency, and investment returns. This project asks a different question: what happens to the people?

What this is

An independent analysis of AI's workforce impact

The AI Human Impact Index is a comprehensive, sector-by-sector analysis of how AI deployment is affecting the workers who occupy and operate commercial real estate spaces around the world. It covers 10 sectors across 15 countries, examining displacement risk, policy gaps, and the human welfare consequences of accelerating automation.

Each country analysis uses a consistent methodology: nine sectors are examined with employment data, automation exposure scores, momentum indicators, and policy gap assessments. The result is a structured picture of where workers are most vulnerable, where transition support exists, and where the gap between technological change and human welfare policy is widest.

This is not a productivity tool. It does not measure efficiency gains or return on AI investment. It measures the human cost, or benefit, of the changes underway. That framing is deliberate and it is the project's core intellectual contribution.

Why it exists

The missing layer in the CRE conversation

Major commercial real estate firms publish AI barometers, readiness indices, and market outlooks. These are useful documents. They are also, almost without exception, framed from the perspective of investors, occupiers, and operating efficiency. The workers whose roles are being automated, restructured, or eliminated rarely appear in these analyses as subjects in their own right.

The AI Human Impact Index is a deliberate counterpoint. It takes the same analytical rigour applied to market data and directs it at workforce impact. It draws on OECD data, national statistics, academic research, and sector-specific analysis to produce assessments that CRE professionals, policymakers, and workers themselves can use to understand what is actually happening.

The intention is not to oppose AI adoption. It is to ensure that the human consequences of that adoption are visible, documented, and part of the conversation.

Guiding principles

How this project approaches its work

  • 1 Human welfare as the primary frame. Every analysis asks first: what does this mean for the people doing this work? Economic efficiency is a secondary consideration.
  • 2 Independence as a differentiator. This project is unaffiliated with any commercial real estate firm, AI vendor, or industry association. That independence is what makes the analysis trustworthy.
  • 3 Transparency over false precision. Where data is uncertain or sources conflict, that is acknowledged openly. Senior audiences deserve honesty about what we know and what we do not.
  • 4 Constructive, not adversarial. The aim is to provide the missing analytical layer, not to position the industry as a villain. Major CRE firms are potential partners in addressing the workforce challenges this project identifies.
  • 5 A living resource. The index is updated quarterly at the data layer and continuously through the Intelligence Feed. It is designed to improve and expand over time, not to be a static publication.
Methodology

How the analysis is built

Each country analysis examines nine sectors: Professional Services, Financial Services, Technology, Retail, Healthcare, Manufacturing, Construction, Logistics and Warehousing, and Public Sector. For each sector, the analysis draws on national employment data, OECD automation exposure research, World Bank labour indicators, and sector-specific industry reports.

The Human Momentum Score (out of 100) measures the direction and pace of AI's impact on workers in each sector, combining automation exposure, policy support, and transition pathway availability. A high score indicates a sector where workers are better placed to navigate AI disruption; a low score indicates high exposure with insufficient support.

Policy gap analysis identifies where national frameworks for worker transition, retraining, and AI governance are insufficient relative to the pace of automation. Source documentation is included throughout, with methodology notes explaining where estimates are used and why.

The core analysis is updated annually. The data layer is updated quarterly as new national statistics and research emerge. Where significant methodological changes are made, these are documented transparently.
