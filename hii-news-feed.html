CoStar Group cuts content and production roles as AI push displaces workers at CRE's largest data firm

CoStar Group confirmed layoffs at its Richmond headquarters in February 2026, with cuts falling primarily on photo, video, and content production teams tied to the Homes.com platform. A company spokesperson said the reductions were directly linked to the deployment of advanced AI technologies intended to streamline operations and accelerate revenue growth, with AI tools taking over functions previously performed by these staff.

Why it matters for CRE
CoStar is the data infrastructure layer beneath most professional CRE decision-making. The fact that its first significant AI-linked workforce reduction hits content and production roles, not administrative back-office functions, signals something worth watching: AI is being deployed to replace the creative and interpretive labour that makes property data usable. This is a pattern the HII has flagged across multiple markets, and it is now visible inside the sector's own data providers. The workers most affected are not the most expensive, but they are often the least protected.

CBRE, JLL, and Cushman & Wakefield stocks fall sharply as investors reprice AI disruption risk for CRE services firms

On 12 February 2026, shares in major CRE services companies fell sharply: CBRE closed down nearly 9%, JLL lost 7.6%, and Cushman & Wakefield slid more than 4%. Bloomberg analysts described the sell-off as an "AI scare trade," with investors rotating out of high-fee, labour-intensive business models on fears that advancing AI automation could undermine the advisory and research revenue these firms depend on. The sell-off came despite strong Q4 earnings from CBRE, including record annual revenue of $40.6 billion.

Why it matters for CRE
The gap between strong current earnings and falling share prices is precisely the signal to watch here. Investors are not reacting to today's results; they are re-pricing the human cost model underlying the entire CRE services industry. Research, valuation, lease abstraction, market analysis — these are the same functions the HII identifies as facing high automation exposure across its sector analysis. CBRE itself acknowledged it aims to cut research costs by approximately 25% using AI. The question for the people who do that work is whether "efficiency" translates to fewer roles, reshaped roles, or simply fewer entry points into the profession.

18 Feb 2026

JLL research finds building adaptability now tied directly to asset value as AI reshapes workforce planning cycles

A new JLL report finds that nearly nine in ten C-suite leaders rank business agility as a top objective, with 85% citing flexible work patterns as a priority and 93% of investors saying technology-enabled properties deliver stronger returns. Hybrid work, AI integration, and accelerating business model change have shortened planning cycles significantly, with organisations increasingly unable to commit to fixed real estate footprints across multi-year horizons. Uncertainty in headcount forecasting, compounded by AI-driven job shifts, is driving demand for elastic portfolio models.

Why it matters for CRE
This is a direct connection between AI's impact on workforce structure and the physical demand for space. When organisations cannot forecast headcount reliably because AI is reshaping roles faster than HR planning can respond, they cannot commit to fixed leases. That instability ripples through the entire occupier market. The workers whose roles are in flux are also the workers whose presence or absence drives space utilisation decisions — yet their welfare is rarely part of that conversation.

14 Jan 2026

IMF research finds AI-related skills lower employment in exposed occupations by 3.6% over five years despite commanding higher wages

A new IMF Staff Discussion Note analysing millions of online job vacancies found that while AI-related skills attract a wage premium of up to 15% in the UK and 8.5% in the US, employment in AI-exposed occupations is 3.6% lower after five years in regions with high demand for AI skills compared to those with less demand. One in ten job postings in advanced economies now requires at least one new skill, with entry-level roles facing the highest exposure and fewest transition pathways. The IMF's managing director warned of an AI "tsunami" particularly affecting younger workers entering the labour market.

Why it matters for CRE
This finding challenges the optimistic framing common in CRE earnings calls, where AI is presented as an augmentation tool with no net employment cost. The IMF data suggests the real picture is more complex: AI skills attract premium pay for those who hold them, but the occupations most exposed to AI see fewer jobs overall over time, not more. For a sector like commercial real estate services, where research, analysis, and advisory functions are high on the automation exposure list, this is a significant medium-term signal. The HII's momentum scores for professional services reflect precisely this two-speed pattern.

20 Feb 2026

Carnegie Endowment warns Europe faces "critical window" as AI labour disruption moves from theoretical to imminent

A Carnegie Endowment for International Peace analysis argues that AI disruption is "unlikely to manifest as sudden mass redundancy" but will instead take the form of incremental task substitution that hollows out roles before eliminating them, creating prolonged insecurity rather than clear-cut unemployment. The German Institute for Employment Research projects 1.6 million jobs could be reshaped or lost to AI in Germany alone over the next fifteen years. The analysis calls for a dedicated European labour transition framework embedded in the EU's 2028-2034 budget, warning that macro employment statistics "tend to lag behind firm-level changes" in ways that could obscure emerging disruption.

Why it matters for CRE
The pattern described here, roles gradually hollowed out rather than cleanly replaced, maps directly onto what is happening in CRE advisory functions. Research compilation, first-draft valuation reports, lease administration and facilities data management are all candidates for this incremental displacement model. Workers in these roles may not receive redundancy notices; they may simply find their workload shrinking and their job grade compressed over time. This is a welfare issue that aggregate statistics will not capture, which is precisely why the HII's sector-level analysis exists.

27 Feb 2026

Companies cited AI in announcing 55,000 job cuts in 2025, twelve times the figure from two years earlier

Data from outplacement firm Challenger, Gray and Christmas shows that in 2025, companies directly attributed 55,000 job cuts to their use of AI, up more than twelvefold from 2023. The majority of those losses were concentrated in tech-heavy sectors, but the pattern is spreading. Oxford Economics analysts have raised the question of whether some firms are using AI adoption as a socially acceptable rationale for layoffs that might otherwise be attributed to overhiring, describing it as "dressing up" workforce reductions as a positive transformation story.

Why it matters for CRE
The narrative framing of AI-linked job cuts matters as much as the numbers. When firms describe redundancies as a strategic AI transition rather than a restructuring, they avoid the reputational and regulatory scrutiny that would otherwise follow. CRE services firms, particularly those with large operational workforces in facilities management and property operations, may adopt similar framing as they rationalise headcount. Workers deserve transparency about whether their role is ending because of genuine technological change or because of cost decisions that use AI as a convenient cover story.

2 Mar 2026

EU AI Act HR obligations take effect in August 2026, requiring human oversight and worker consultation before deploying high-risk systems

Legal analysis published in early March 2026 confirms that many AI tools deployed in HR functions, including candidate screening, performance monitoring, and task allocation systems, will be classified as high-risk under the EU AI Act when full obligations come into force in August 2026. Employers must establish meaningful human oversight, inform and consult employee representatives before deployment, and ensure supervisors have genuine capacity to intervene in AI decisions. The European Commission's Digital Omnibus package is under discussion and may extend some deadlines to December 2027 or August 2028.

Why it matters for CRE
For global CRE services firms with European operations, this is a compliance timeline that is arriving faster than most HR functions are prepared for. Facilities management and property operations divisions that use AI for scheduling, performance monitoring, or workforce allocation sit directly in scope. The "meaningful human oversight" requirement is not a box-ticking exercise; it creates genuine organisational accountability for AI decisions affecting workers. Firms that have not yet audited their HR AI tools should be treating August 2026 as an urgent deadline.

28 Feb 2026

Commercial Observer survey: most CRE firms frame AI as augmentation rather than replacement, but fund efficiency gains through reduced outsourcing

A Commercial Observer survey of CRE firms in February 2026 found that most report deploying AI to eliminate manual work and accelerate analysis rather than reduce headcount directly. However, the survey also found that a majority of firms are funding AI investment primarily through reduced outsourcing and administrative costs, not workforce cuts. A majority of surveyed firms plan to meaningfully increase AI spending over the next two years, with over half expecting to purchase new AI software within twelve months.

Why it matters for CRE
The "augmentation not replacement" framing appears consistent until you read the funding mechanism. Reducing outsourcing and administrative costs is another way of describing the elimination of contracted and support roles, people who work in the sector even if not on the main headcount. The welfare question is the same regardless of employment classification: whose work disappears, and what happens to those people? This survey reflects an industry confident in its own narrative of responsible transition, and that confidence deserves scrutiny.

43% of workers are trying to change career fields in 2026, with AI-linked layoffs and workplace anxiety driving the highest rate in years

New research from employment platform FlexJobs finds that more than four in ten workers, 43%, are actively trying to change career fields in 2026, a figure the platform describes as among the highest it has recorded. AI-linked layoffs and anxiety about automation vulnerability are cited among the primary drivers, alongside broader uncertainty about the stability of existing roles. The study captures a workforce in significant psychological transition, with workers across professional services and administrative functions expressing doubt about the durability of their current career paths.

Why it matters for CRE
Workforce anxiety is a real estate occupancy signal. When a significant share of the professional workforce is actively reconsidering its career direction, the demand patterns for office space, and the kind of spaces workers want, shift alongside those decisions. But there is also a direct welfare implication for CRE's own workforce: research analysts, facilities coordinators, valuation assistants, and property management professionals are all in roles the HII has flagged as having above-average automation exposure. The career change impulse may well be most acute among exactly these workers.

The question is not whether AI will reshape knowledge work in commercial real estate, but whether the firms involved are preparing their people rather than simply their technology stacks. The HII's analysis of North American and European markets consistently finds that the policy and transition support infrastructure lags well behind the pace of deployment.
